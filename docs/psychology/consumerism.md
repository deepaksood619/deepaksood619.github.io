## [Conspicuous Consumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption)

The spending of money on and the acquiring of luxury goods and services to publicly display economic power." (related: [Veblen goods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veblen_good) - "types of luxury goods, such as expensive wines, jewellery, fashion-designer handbags, and luxury cars, which are in demand because of the high prices asked for them.")

A classic example of this would be a luxury watch: A Rolex isn't better at telling the time than a cheap Casio -- but a Rolex signals something about its owner's economic power and thus their social standing.

## Planned Obsolescence

In [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics") and [industrial design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_design "Industrial design"), **planned obsolescence** (also called **built-in obsolescence** or **premature obsolescence**) is the concept of policies planning or designing a [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_\(economics\) "Good (economics)") with an artificially limited [useful life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_lifetime "Product lifetime") or a purposely frail design, so that it becomes [obsolete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obsolete "Obsolete") after a certain predetermined period of time upon which it decrementally functions or suddenly ceases to function, or might be perceived as [unfashionable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fashion "Fashion"). Once regarded as a [conspiracy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conspiracy_theory "Conspiracy theory"), the rationale behind this strategy is to generate long-term sales volume by reducing the time between repeat purchases (referred to as "shortening the replacement cycle"). It is the deliberate shortening of the lifespan of a product to force people to purchase functional replacements.
